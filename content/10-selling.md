# Selling and getting paid

You can build the most beautiful thing in the world. If nobody pays for it, it's just art.

This chapter is about the unglamorous part: pricing, finding customers, getting money into your bank account. The part most "learn to code" content skips entirely.

## Pricing (the rule that took me years to learn)

**Charge more than feels comfortable.**

Whatever number you first think of — double it. Then charge that.

People assume cheap = bad. €29/month feels more credible than €4/month. The €4 customer is also the most demanding one. They will email you twice a week asking for refunds and new features. The €29 customer will smile, pay, and use it quietly for two years.

A useful sanity check: divide your price by the time it saves them, per month. If you save someone two hours a month and you charge €30/month, you're charging €15/hour of their time. That's a steal. They will pay happily.

## The first 10 customers — the only hard part

Forget marketing. Forget Instagram. The first 10 customers come from exactly one of three places:

### 1. People you know who have the problem

Mom. Friends' parents. Teachers. The owner of the café you go to. The dance studio. Don't be shy. *"Hey, I built this thing. Want to try it free for 30 days? If it saves you time, you can pay me €X/month after."*

That single sentence — *"want to try it free for 30 days?"* — is the most powerful sales sentence in software.

### 2. Communities where they hang out

Reddit (subreddits for your niche), Discord, niche Facebook groups, local business networks (especially in Austria — *Wirtschaftskammer* communities).

**Rule:** don't spam. Be helpful first. Answer questions for two weeks. Then, when relevant, mention what you built. *"I actually built a thing for this — happy to give a free account."*

### 3. Cold outreach with a real demo

Pick 10 people who have the problem. Build the thing *specifically for them* (you only need to add their logo/name to the demo). Send a 90-second Loom video:

> *"Hi [name], I'm Katherina. I'm 17 and I build small things on the internet. I noticed [specific thing about their business] and thought I could help. Here's a 90-second demo of a tool I made — it's already set up with your business name. If you like it, you can use it free for 30 days, no card needed."*

1 in 10 will say yes. That's enough to start.

## What happens after the first sale

Three things happen, in roughly this order:

1. **You feel weird.** Real money for something you typed at a computer feels like cheating. It isn't. You provided value. They paid for it. That's the entire economy.

2. **You want to make it perfect.** Resist. Make it slightly better. Then go find customer two with the version you have.

3. **You realize you can do it again.** This is the real prize. Not the money — the proof. Once you know you can do it, you can do it many more times, in many different niches, for the rest of your life.

The first customer is the proof. Everything after is just practice.
